Kirchoff Matrix-Tree Theorem

A spanning subgraph of a graph \( X = (V, E) \) is a subgraph whose vertex set is exactly \( V \), obtained by deleting some (possibly none) edges. A spanning tree of \( X \) is a spanning subgraph that is also a tree, i.e., connected and acyclic. We denote by \( \tau(X) \) the number of spanning trees of \( X \); in particular, \( X \) is connected if and only if \( \tau(X) > 0 \). The problem of counting spanning trees is central in graph theory, and in this article we study the Kirchhoff Matrix Tree Theorem, which gives a matrix-based method to compute \( \tau(X) \).

Our goal is to compute \( \tau(X) \), the number of spanning trees of a connected graph \( X \). Rather than enumerating all possible trees directly, which quickly becomes infeasible, we seek a systematic and efficient method to determine this quantity.

The contraction of a graph \( X \) by an edge \( e \) with endpoints \( x \) and \( y \) is the graph obtained by merging \( x \) and \( y \) into a single vertex. All edges that were incident to \( x \) or \( y \), except \( e \) itself, remain incident to the new vertex. The resulting multigraph, denoted \( X/e \), has one fewer edge than \( X \).

Theorem. Let \( X \) be a graph. If \( e \) is an edge of \( X \) that is not a loop, then \[ \tau(X) = \tau(X - e) + \tau(X / e) \].

Proof. The number of spanning trees \( \tau(X) \) of \( X \) equals the sum of the number of spanning trees that do not contain \( e \) and the number of spanning trees that do contain \( e \). The spanning trees of \( X \) that omit \( e \) are counted by \( \tau(X - e) \). The family of spanning trees that contain \( e \) is in bijective correspondence with the set of spanning trees of \( X / e \). When we contract \( e \) in a spanning tree that contains \( e \), we obtain a spanning tree of \( X / e \) because the resulting subgraph is spanning, connected, and has the correct number of edges. Since other edges maintain their identity under contraction, no two trees map to the same spanning tree of \( X / e \), and each spanning tree of \( X \) arises this way. This establishes the theorem.

Now that we have seen the recursive relation for counting spanning trees, we shall move on to the main theorem — the Kirchhoff Matrix Tree Theorem — which provides a closed-form formula to compute \( \tau(X) \) directly using matrices associated with the graph.

For a matrix \( M \), we denote the entry in the \( i \)-th row and \( j \)-th column by \( M[i,j] \).

Theorem 5.3.2 (Kirchhoff’s Matrix-Tree Theorem). Let \( X \) be a graph with vertex set \([n]\) for some \( n \ge 1 \) and Laplacian matrix \( L \). Then:
  1. For any \( i \in [n] \), \( \tau(X) = \det L[i] \).
  2. For any \( i,j \in [n] \), \( \tau(X) = (-1)^{i+j} \det L[i,j] \).
  3. \( \mathrm{adj}(L) = \tau(X) J_n \).
  4. \( \tau(X) = n^{-2} \det(J_n + L) \).
  5. If \( 0 = \mu_1 \le \mu_2 \le \dots \le \mu_n \) are the eigenvalues of \( L \), then \( \tau(X) = \frac{\mu_2 \cdot \dots \cdot \mu_n}{n} \).

Proof. Clearly, part (2) is a generalization of part (1). For part (1), we use strong induction on the number of edges. Let \( e = ij \) be an edge of \( X \). Denote by \( E \) the \(([n] \setminus \{i\}) \times ([n] \setminus \{i\})\) matrix with all zeros except \( E[j,j] = 1 \). Then \( L[i] = L(X-e)[i] + E \), which implies \[ \det L[i] = \det L(X-e)[i] + \det L(X-e)[i,j] = \det L(X-e)[i] + \det L[i,j]. \] Contracting \( e \) gives \( L(X/e)[j] = L[i,j] \), and by induction hypothesis, \( \det L(X-e)[i] = \tau(X-e) \), \( \det L(X/e)[i] = \tau(X/e) \). Using Theorem 5.3.1, we get \( \det L[i] = \tau(X-e) + \tau(X/e) = \tau(X) \), proving part (1). The remaining parts follow by linear algebra properties of Laplacian and adjacency matrices as described in the theorem statement.
